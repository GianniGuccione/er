\section{Summary and Conclusions}

This report evaluates torsional vibration of a Cummins QSB 6.7 L six-cylinder diesel engine directly coupled to an ultra-high-pressure triplex plunger pump.

Cummins provides the QSB 6.7 L diesel engine rated at 275 hp. Torque is transmitted through a Pitts electric clutch, spline shaft, TCP torsional damper, and 5:1 reduction gearbox. NLB provides the remaining driveline components and the CV300 ultra-high-pressure triplex plunger pump.

Overload of the torsion damper springs and engine-side clutch bearing failures are the motivation for this evaluation. Measurements of spline shaft torque are recorded with torsional strain gauges and compared to predicted torque from a mathematical model.

\[
\text{Engine}
\rightarrow
\text{Clutch}
\rightarrow
\text{Spline Shaft}
\rightarrow
\text{Damper}
\rightarrow
\text{5:1 Reduction Gear}
\rightarrow
\text{Pump}
\]

The maximum spline shaft torque occurs near 1157 RPM. Operation above approximately 1400 RPM is recommended for this particular torsion damper spring rate and rotating inertia. Life and reliability can be improved by supporting the engine-side of the spline shaft with a pilot bearing located in the engine flywheel, evaluating revised torsion spring rates, and investigating viscous torsional dampers.

\section{General}

\subsection{Summary}

\subsubsection*{Objectives}

\begin{itemize}
\item Determine vibratory torque produced by a Cummins QSB 6.7 L six-cylinder diesel engine driving a CV300 triplex pump through a 4.93:1 reduction gearbox.
\item Determine clutch bearing loads resulting from coupling torque fluctuations, spline shaft misalignment, and shaft form deviation.
\item Measure spline shaft torque and bending using telemetry-based strain gauge instrumentation.
\item Compare analytical predictions with measured results.
\item Develop recommendations to improve clutch and driveline reliability.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Model Assumptions}

\begin{itemize}
\item The drivetrain is represented using a lumped-parameter torsional model.
\item Engine and pump torque inputs are generated from slider-crank kinematics and dynamic force analysis.
\item The coupling is represented by equivalent torsional stiffness and damping.
\item Reflected inertia is used to represent the pump-side drivetrain.
\item Damping is assumed viscous for frequency-response and transient analyses.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Solutions}

\subsubsection*{Clutch Bearing Solutions}

\begin{itemize}
\item Support the spline shaft using a pilot bearing within the engine flywheel.
\item Improve shaft alignment and spline fit quality.
\item Evaluate spherical roller bearing arrangements capable of accommodating angular misalignment.
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{Images/pilot_bearing_concept.png}
\caption{Pilot bearing concept used to support the engine-side of the spline shaft.}
\label{fig:pilot_bearing_concept}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection*{Torsion Damper Solutions}

\begin{itemize}
\item Avoid operation near torsional resonance.
\item Evaluate viscous torsional damper designs.
\item Evaluate alternative torsion spring rates.
\item Investigate centrifugal pendulum absorbers for low-speed operation.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Lumped Parameter Torsional System}

The drivetrain is modeled as a two-inertia torsional system. The engine-side inertia is represented by $J_1$, and the reflected pump-side inertia is represented by $J_{2eq}$. The spline shaft and torsion damper are represented by equivalent stiffness and damping terms.

\subsection{Engine and Pump Kinematic Analysis}

This section develops the slider-crank position, velocity, and acceleration relationships used to determine piston motion, connecting rod motion, inertia forces, and input torque requirements.

\begin{equation}
x = r\cos\theta + \sqrt{l^2-r^2\sin^2(\theta)}
\label{eq:x}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
K=\sqrt{l^2-r^2\sin^2(\theta)}
\label{eq:K}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\dot{x}
=
r\dot{\theta}\sin\theta
\left(
-1-\frac{r\cos\theta}{K}
\right)
\label{eq:xdot}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\ddot{x}
=
-\frac{
r(K+r\cos\theta)
\left(
K^2\ddot{\theta}\cos\theta
+
K^2\dot{\theta}^{\,2}\sin\theta
+
r\dot{\theta}^{\,2}
(-K+r\cos\theta)\sin^2\theta
\right)
}
{K^3}
\label{eq:xddot}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\phi
=
\tan^{-1}
\left(
-\frac{r\sin\theta}{K}
\right)
\label{eq:phi}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\dot{\phi}
=
-\frac{r\dot{\theta}\cos\theta}{K}
\label{eq:phidot}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\ddot{\phi}
=
\frac{
-K^2r\ddot{\theta}\cos\theta
+
r\dot{\theta}^{\,2}
\left(
K^2-r^2\cos^2\theta
\right)
\sin\theta
}
{K^3}
\label{eq:phiddot}
\end{equation}

\section{Conclusions}

\subsection{Findings}

The torsional system exhibits a primary resonance near 1157 RPM. Operation near this speed range increases coupling vibratory torque and may contribute to torsion damper spring overload and clutch bearing damage.

\subsection{Recommendations}

Operation above approximately 1400 RPM is recommended for this torsion damper configuration. Additional improvements include engine-side spline shaft support, improved alignment control, revised torsion spring rates, and evaluation of viscous torsional damping.
